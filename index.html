About me

I earned my PhD in Rhetoric, Composition, and the Teaching of English at the University of Arizona. My research focues using interdisciplinary researc skills to developed ethical, community engaged research practices. I design and teach courses in Technical and Professional Writing, including Writing for the Web, UX Research and Testing, Technical Editing, and Web Development.

As an instructor, my classes emphasize accessibility, user experience, and collaborative workflows. I often build in service-learning projects so students can work with real clients and institutional partners.Across all of my courses, I encourage students to connect theory with practice and to develop professional portfolios that highlight their growth. I strongly believe students need exposure to a balance of digital literacy skills, and ethical frameworks grounded in critical theory.

CV and Academic Research

As a researcher and teacher, I focus on work that supports meaningful community engagement. I strongly believe in open-access, community-engaged scholarship that unites several disciplines and bridges academic and community spaces. My research specializes in the rhetoric of collective memory, and it is informed by coursework in film studies and my background in digital media. In particular, my research examines how rhetorical practices of public memory foster empowerment, resistance, and recognition of marginalized communities. My dissertation, Into Space Filmbook: Public Memory in New Mexico's Central Mining District, was awarded an honorable mention at CCCC this year for the James Berlin Outstanding Dissertation Award. The project applies feminist rhetorical methods and a digital humanities approach to an oral history case study investigating how a group of people maintain their sense of community despite a loss of home. As a result of this research, I have published “The Shrine of Chino Mine: Extraction Rhetoric and Public Memory in Southern New Mexico” in Xchanges. I am also currently revising my webtext “Shaping Narratives: Exploring A Hybrid Documentary Methodology,” which was accepted to Kairos: A Journal of Rhetoric, Technology, and Pedagogy. This article highlights how cultural and digital rhetorics create a fruitful intersection for rhetorical scholarship.

Teaching Portfolio

My teaching philosophy is rooted in the belief that classrooms serve as spaces for fostering community and preparing students for their various careers. In particular, my classes focus on ethical decision-making in both writing and design. I also place an emphasis on students learning to collaborate on teams using the agile workflow, as well as teaching students how to give and receive critical feedback in a variety of contexts. I have recently taught several technical rhetoric courses, including Introduction to Technical and Professional Writing, Technical Editing, Media Theory & Production, UX Research & Design, Writing for the Web, and Web Development. Whenever I can, I structure my classes as service-learning courses so that students gain hands-on experience working with clients. In all of my courses, I teach students to research career opportunities in their fields and prepare portfolios for prospective employers.
